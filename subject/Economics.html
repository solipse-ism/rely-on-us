      • Fiscal Policy: the use of government spending and taxation to influenece economic activity and achieve macroeconomic objectives:
        • Budget: the government financial plans in term of revenue and spending

            • Tax revenue: direct and indirect tax
            • Non tax revenue: fines, loans, and tolls

            • Education
            • Healthcare

          • Classification of budget
            • Budget deficit (government spending > government revenue)
            • Budget surplus (government spending < government revenue)
            • Balanced Budget (government spending = government revenue)
        • Taxation
          • Direct tax: tax on income eg. income tax, corporation tax
          • Indirect tax: tax on spending eg. VAT (sales), customs tax (import)
          • Classification of tax
            • Progressive: tax rise as income rise (tax on rich)
            • Regressive: tax rate fall as income rise (tax on poor)
            • Proportional: tax is unchanged as income rise (rich and poor equal)
          • Reason for taxation
            • to fund government spending
            • to protect domestic firm
            • to redistribute income and wealth
            • correct market failure eg. inflation
          • Impact of taxation
            • Tax raise cost of production
            • Decrease in supply, contraction in demand
            • Equilibrium price rise, quantity falls
            • Tax burden:
              • Tax per unit: bd
              • Consumer pay: bc
              • Producer pay: cd
            • Decrease demand for demerit goods
          • Impact lower taxation
            • disposable income
            • incentive + investment + efficiency
            • resource depletion
            • import spending
            • government revenue decrease
            • attract MNCs and FDI
            • discourage tax avoidance
          • Principle of Taxation
            • Convenience: method of payment
            • Equitable: (fairness) should be on base tax payer ability to pay
            • Economical: easy and cheap to collect
            • Flexibility: taxes must be able to adapt to changes in economy
            • Efficiency: tax system should try to achieve its aims without causing inefficiency
            • Certainty: tax payer should know what/when/where/how to play tax
        • Type of Fiscal Policy
          • Expansionary
            • reduce indirect and direct tax, increase government spending
          • Contractionary
            • increase indirect and direct tax, reduce government spending
      • Monetary Policy: the use of interest rate, money supply and exchange rates to achieve macroeconomic aims
        • Type of Monetary Policy
          • Expansionary
            • measures: decrease in interest, or increase in money supply
            • effect: decrease saving due to low return, increase borrowing due to low cost, fall in exchange rate due to less hot money
          • Contractionay
            • everything opposite
        • Limitation of Monetary Policy
          • Time lag (between interest changes and impact on the economy)
          • Impact depend on other factor such as confidence and fiscal policy
          • Conflict between maco aims
            • eg. higher interest rate reduce inflation but also reduce economic growth
      • Supply-side Policy: long term policiers to increase productive capacity of the economy leading to an out ward shift of the PPC
        • Measures
          • More / better education and training ⇒ Skills of the labour force increases ⇒ Labour mobility increases ⇒ Labour productivity rises ⇒ PPC shifts outwards
          • Government reduces income tax ⇒ Workers earn more disposable income ⇒ Greater incentives to work ⇒ Quantity of labour increases ⇒ PPC shifts outwards
          • Government reduces corporation tax ⇒ Firms have more retained profit ⇒ Increased investment ⇒ Rise in the quantity / quality of capital and labour ⇒ PPC shifts outwards
          • Deregulation (reduce rules) ⇒ Removal / reduction of barriers to entry ⇒ Markets are more competitive ⇒ Lower prices and higher quality / quantity of output ⇒ PPC shifts outwards
          • Privatisation of government enterprises ⇒ Private sector is profit motivated ⇒ Increased efficiency and productivity ⇒ Lower prices and higher quality / quantity of output ⇒ PPC shifts outwards
          • Reduce / remove minimum wages, TU power and legislation ⇒ Cheaper and easier to hire & fire ⇒ Increased labour market flexibility / efficiency ⇒ Lower unemployment (i.e. increase in labour) ⇒ PPC shifts outwards
        • Impact on macro aims
          • Sustainable economic growth
          • Increase employment
          • More stable price, less demand pull or cost push inflation
          • Increased international competitiveness ⇒ boost export, reduce import, improve BoPs
          • Better education low inequality
      • Economic growth: an increase in the national output (GDP) or an increase in productive capacity of an economy over a period of time(1year)

          • Measured by the % change in GDP.
          • GDP is rising (positive growth rate)
          • More output can be produced
          • PPC shifts outwards
        • Gross Domestic Product (GDP): the total market value of all goods and services produced in an economy in one year
          • Types of GDP
            • Real GDP: Adjusted for inflation
            • Nominal GDP: Unadjusted for inflation
            • GDP per capita: GDP divided by population
          • Components of GDP
            • GDP = Consumption + Investment + Government spending + (Exports - Imports)
            • GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)
              • Consumption: spending by households on goods and services
              • Investment: spending by firms on capital goods
              • Government spending: consumption and investment by the government
              • Exports: goods and services produced domestically and sold abroad
              • Imports: goods and services produced abroad and sold domestically
        • Causes of economic growth
          • Factor endowment: the quality / quantity of resources (the FOPs)
          • The labour force: size, skill, mobility
          • Labour productivity (output per worker per time period): training, education, experience, qualifications, motivation, machinery, technology
          • Capital investment: spending on capital goods (machinery)
          • Investment in infrastructure: roads, railways, schools, hospital
          • Technological change: improvements in capital equipment
        • Consequences
        • Policies to promote economic growth

          • expansionary monetary policy
          • supply-side policy

            • Causes of recession

      • Employment and unemployment
        • employment: the use of labour as a factor of production in the economy
          • sector:
            • Primary, Secondary, Tertiary
          • benefits
            • Increased living standards
            • Promotes economic growth
            • Increases tax revenue
            • Less welfare spending
            • Prevents “brain drain” (skilled workers moving abroad)
            • Reduces income and wealth inequality
        • full employment: everyone in a country who is willing and able to work has a job
        • unemployment: when people of working age are willing and able to work but cannot find employment (a job)
          • measuring unemployment
            • claimant count: all the employed people who are receiving welfare payments from the government
            • labour force survey: an unemployment survey carried out by the International Labour Orgainization (ILO)
            • unemployment rate: the number of unemployed people as a percentage by the labour force
          • type of unemployment
            • Frictional: when workers are unemployed for a short period of time as they search for a new job (they are in between jobs).
            • Cyclical: when there is a downturn/recession in the economic cycle, resulting in less aggregate demand for goods and services. This results in less demand for workers.
            • Structural: As economies develop, the structure of the economy changes. Some workers will lose jobs in one sector and are unable to find work in a different sector because they lack the necessary skills.
            • Seasonal: At different times of the year workers might lose their jobs because there is no demand for what they offer
          • Policies to reduce unemployment
            • Fiscal policy:
              • Increase government spending on public sector employment (e.g. government workers) and infrastructure (e.g. roads, schools, hospitals)→increases AD ↔ increases output ↔ increases employment.
              • Lower direct taxes (e.g. income tax)→more disposable income ↔ more spending ↔ increases output ↔ increases employment.
              • Lower indirect taxes→prices fall ↔ more spending ↔ firms increase output ↔ lower unemployment.
            • Monetary policy:
              • The central bank lowers interest rates or increases the money supply→discourages saving and encourages borrowing ↔ more spending ↔ more output by firms ↔ reduces unemployment.
            • Supply-side policy:
              • Education and training programmes→increases skills ↔ easier to become employed.
              • Lower income taxes→keep more disposable income ↔ incentive for unemployed to find a job.
              • Decrease in welfare payments (e.g. unemployment benefits)→unemployed people receive less money from the government ↔ incentive to work.
            • Protectionist policies:
              • Governments use tariffs and quotas→increases the the price of imports ↔ consumers switch to domestic products ↔ more demand ↔ more output ↔ more employment.
        • change patterns of employment
          • Change in employment sector
            • economic develop ⇒ secondary and tertiary increase, primary decrease
          • Delayed entry to workforce due to education
          • Aging population
            • people live longer, more aged worker in labour force
          • Formal sector employment
            • more work in formal, less in informal
          • Female participation
          • Public sector decrease, Private sector increase
          • Flexible working pattern

        • Consumer price index (CPI): a weighted index of consumer prices in the economy over time. It is used to measure the cost of living for an average household.
          • calculation:
        • Income and inflation

          • Real income: adjusted for the effects of inflation.
          • Calculate: Nominal income - Inflation rate (%) = Real income
        • Type of inflation
          • demand pull inflation: when demand exceeds the supply
          • Cost Push inflation: when cost of production increases by a lot
          • imported inflation:when prices of imported raw materials cause higher costs of production for domestic producers.
          • Monetary inflation: when increases in the money supply cause prices to rise
        • benign (good) inflation - when costs of production fall resulting in a lower price level.
        • malign (bad) inflation - when aggregate (total) demand falls, causing the general price level to fall

    • 6. International trade and globalisation
      • International specialisation - occurs when individuals, firms, regions or countries concentrate on the production of a particular good or service.
        • national level - superior resource allocation and/or cheaper production methods e.g. silicon valley
          • Advantage
            • Increased efficiency
            • Increased labour productivity
            • Increased productive capacity
            • Economies of scale
            • Improved competitiveness
            • Increased trade
          • Disadvantage
            • Over-specialisation - can't react to chang
            • Lack of variety (choice) for consumers
            • High labour turnover - workers might get bored and seek other opportunities. Industries with high labour turnover often pay the lowest wages.
            • Low labour mobility - structural unemployment
            • High labour costs - as highly skill
      • Globalisation - the process by which the world’s economies become increasingly interdependent and interconnected. Free trade -The absence of protectionist measures (trade barriers) e.g. no tariffs or quotas. Protection - the use of trade barriers to restrict international trade and limit foreign competition.
        • Advantage Globalisation
          • Increased international trade, which creates jobs and wealth
          • Firms exploit economies of scale(lower average costs as scale of production increases).
          • Greater choice of goods and services for consumers.
          • Freer movement of labour, capital, goods and services.
          • Greater cultural understanding and appreciation.
        • Disadvantage Globalisation
          • Increased wealth and income inequality.
          • Over-dependence on the global economy.
        • MNCs - a business that operates in two or more countries.
          • Factor that allow MNCs to exist
